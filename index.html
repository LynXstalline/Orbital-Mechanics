The Physics of Balance
Core Concept
Think of it like this
You know when you swing a ball on a string above your head and the string keeps it from flying off? Gravity does the same thing for satellites. It constantly pulls the satellite back toward Earth, kind of like an invisible string. As long as the satellite is moving fast enough sideways, it keeps looping around and never actually lands.

So here is the wild thing about satellites. They are literally falling toward Earth the whole time, every second. But because they are also moving sideways really fast, by the time they "fall" a little bit, the Earth has curved away underneath them. So they just keep missing. That is what an orbit actually is. It is non-stop falling and missing at the same time.

Try cranking up the Planet Mass slider and you can see the satellite immediately has to work harder to maintain its orbit. A heavier planet means stronger gravity, so the satellite needs more speed to keep circling instead of spiraling inward.

F = G x (m1 x m2) / r²
F = the gravitational force between two objects
G = a constant number that never changes in nature (6.674 x 10⁻¹¹)
m1 and m2 = the masses of both objects
r² = distance squared — double the distance = 4x weaker pull

Too Slow

Gravity wins the tug of war. The satellite spirals inward and eventually burns up coming through the atmosphere. Drag the velocity slider way down to watch this happen.

Just Right

Gravity and speed perfectly balance each other out. The orbit stays stable. This is the exact point engineers need to hit when launching a real satellite.

Too Fast

The satellite has so much energy that gravity cannot hold it anymore. It escapes and flies off into deep space. No coming back from that one.

The Calculus of Motion
Rate of Change

Calculus sounds way more scary than it actually is. At its core, it is just the math of things that change over time. Motion is literally all about things changing, so it makes sense that calculus is the tool we use to understand it.

Quick analogy
Think about being in a car. Where you are on the road is your position. How fast that position is changing is your velocity, basically the speedometer. And how fast your velocity is changing is acceleration, like pressing the gas pedal. Calculus connects all three of those together.
01
Position r(t) is just where the satellite is at any given moment. The (t) part means it changes depending on time, which makes sense because the satellite is always moving.
02
Velocity = dr/dt is how fast the position is changing. In the simulation, the blue arrow on the satellite is literally showing you this. The direction it points is where the satellite is headed.
03
Acceleration = dv/dt is how fast the velocity is changing. For the satellite, gravity is what causes this. It constantly bends the path back toward Earth, which keeps the orbit curved instead of going straight.
v = sqrt( G x M / r )
This gives the exact one speed needed for a stable circular orbit at a given radius. The bigger r is, the slower the required speed. Engineers use this exact formula when planning where to put satellites, because getting it wrong is an extremely expensive mistake.

English: Literature & Language
Literary Analysis
The Martian
Andy Weir, 2011

Andy Weir's The Martian (2011) is probably the most satellite-relevant piece of fiction that exists. In the book, astronaut Mark Watney gets stranded alone on Mars after his crew has to evacuate during a storm. For a long time, everyone back on Earth thinks he is dead. The thing that proves he is still alive? A NASA satellite analyst named Mindy Park notices that solar panels and equipment have moved in orbital images of the landing site. Without a satellite watching from above, Watney stays dead as far as the world is concerned.

Weir spent years doing research before writing a single word. He studied orbital mechanics and spaceflight history, and he actually wrote custom software to simulate the orbital trajectories in the novel. That is basically the same math we are using in the simulation on this page.

"I'm pretty much done for. That's my honest assessment of the situation. Completely, totally done for."
— paraphrase of Mark Watney's opening log, The Martian, Ch. 1 (Andy Weir, 2011)

From an English perspective, one of the most interesting things Weir does in this book is the format. Almost the whole thing is written as Watney's personal log entries, in first person and present tense. This is not an accident. It puts the reader right inside his head, and it makes the science feel personal instead of like a textbook. Watney cracks jokes while doing life-or-death chemistry, and that contrast is actually a really deliberate choice.

The technical writing in the novel mixes two registers at once: formal scientific language and casual everyday speech. When Watney describes a problem, he uses real orbital mechanics terminology, but he delivers it the way someone would describe what they had for breakfast. That is a hard thing to pull off, and it is a big part of why the book got so popular with people who are not normally science fiction readers.

Satellites as Eyes

NASA's orbital imaging satellites are the tool that reveals Watney is alive. Weir uses satellite tech as a plot device, the all-seeing eye bridging a man 140 million miles away from the people trying to save him.

Science as Story

The same orbital formulas we use in our Physics section appear in the novel as life-or-death decisions. Weir embeds real calculations into the plot rather than explaining them separately.

Voice and Register

Watney's log entries mix technical precision with casual humor. This switching between formal and informal registers is a deliberate literary technique that makes hard science feel readable.

Is One Life Worth It?

A central tension in the novel is whether one person's life is worth the risk of a rescue that could kill others. This connects directly to the cost-benefit question in our Economics section.

Another thing worth noting is how Weir handles the character's tone under pressure. Watney writes "I need to figure out how to grow food on a planet where nothing grows" in the same flat, matter-of-fact way someone would write a to-do list. That calmness is itself a stylistic choice: it signals competence, it creates dark humor, and it keeps the reader from panicking alongside him. Weir uses understatement as a narrative tool the whole way through the book.

Economics: Cost-Benefit Analysis of Satellites
Applied Economics

Satellites are not just a physics or engineering problem. They are also an economics one. Every satellite that gets launched costs hundreds of millions of dollars to build, launch, and keep running. And every decision about whether to build one, who gets to use it, and who cleans it up when it breaks comes down to cost-benefit analysis.

What is cost-benefit analysis?
Cost-benefit analysis is when you add up all the good things something produces and compare them to all the bad things (the costs). If the benefits are bigger than the costs, it is probably worth doing. If not, it probably is not. For satellites, it gets complicated fast because the benefits often go to millions of people who never paid for the satellite in the first place.

So GPS alone has generated roughly $1.4 trillion in economic value for the US since 1983, according to a 2019 study by RTI International commissioned by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST). About 90% of that came after 2010, right when smartphones took off. The initial GPS constellation cost around $12 billion to build. So the return on investment is enormous.

Weather satellites are another big one. NOAA's satellite data contributes to over $30 billion in annual economic value through weather forecasting in the US alone. A 2024 study published in Weather, Climate, and Society estimated that the current constellation of Earth-observing satellites saves more than 1,000 lives per year from severe weather events. In terms of economic value, that works out to over $12 billion annually just from lives saved, which is more than the entire US contribution to those satellites costs per year.

The tricky thing about all this is what economists call externalities. GPS was built entirely by the US military and now gets used for free by billions of people every day. Those billions of people are getting a benefit they never paid for. That is a positive externality, and satellites are full of them.

Now the negative externality: space debris. Based on ESA's 2024 Space Environment Report, there are around 31,000 tracked objects in orbit, of which roughly 9,300 are actually working satellites. The rest is junk. And beyond what we can track, models estimate over 1.2 million pieces of debris between 1 and 10 centimeters, which is big enough to destroy a satellite on impact but too small to track. Every company that launches satellites and leaves broken ones behind is creating a cost for everyone else.

In the 500-600km altitude band where most commercial satellites operate, ESA estimates satellites face nearly 30 close-call events with other objects per year. Each of those requires an avoidance maneuver, which uses up fuel and shortens the satellite's life. Somebody pays for that, and it is not the company that left the debris up there.

The Martian connects here too
In the novel, NASA faces a real cost-benefit question: is one person's life worth the risk of a rescue mission that could cost billions and potentially kill the rescue crew? Economists actually have a number for how they value a human life in policy decisions, called the "Value of a Statistical Life." In the US it is around $11 million per person (US Department of Transportation, 2023). A Mars rescue would cost orders of magnitude more than that. The fact that NASA decides to go anyway is kind of a reminder that not everything can be reduced to pure numbers.

The honest takeaway from all of this is that satellites are one of the best investments humanity has ever made. The returns are massive, especially when you compare the cost of building GPS to what the world gets out of it every year. But the distribution of those returns is nowhere near equal: wealthy countries with strong tech infrastructure capture most of the value, while the countries that arguably need it most, like those dealing with food insecurity or disaster risk, often lack the tools to actually use the data. That is where cost-benefit analysis stops being purely about money and starts being about what kind of world we want.
